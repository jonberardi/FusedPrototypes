Three priorities, one shape

Each starts with the pain in your words, then the move most teams reach for, and why that move typically doesn't work (with some EE specific data points supporting why).

The pain

The number is always under pressure. Sellers are grinding, and there is never enough qualified pipeline to feel good about the quarter.

What most teams reach for

More volume. More SDRs, more outbound, more conference presence, an intent-data layer, maybe an AI SDR. If pipeline is short, make more of it.

More volume only helps if conversion holds, and here it does not. Pouring more leads into a 2.4% motion does not make revenue, it makes cost, and it burns the seller hours that could go to the 56.5% cross-sell motion into your existing base. Whether that 2.4% is a broken acquisition motion or a deliberate plant-the-flag motion that belongs outside the forecast, the capacity question is the same: that effort is producing almost no recognized revenue, and it is time not spent where you already win. The constraint isn't lead count. The targeting is off, or winnable deals are worked the wrong way. More pipeline at the same conversion is a bigger pile of the same problem.

What this means

Pipeline generation is not a volume problem, it is a targeting problem. The best pipeline is in two places: the existing base, and the accounts actually in a buying window right now.

How we'd help
  • Point sellers at the accounts actually in a buying window, so the next batch of pipeline converts at something better than 2.4%.
  • Open new opportunities inside the existing base, where you already win 56.5%, instead of pouring more cost into the prospect motion.
  • Sharpen who gets pursued, so seller hours go to winnable deals rather than volume that ends in nearly three of four wins at zero dollars.
The pain

You walk into a deal review and the forecast is mush. Deals that were happening slip a quarter, then another, then quietly die. You cannot trust the data, so you cannot trust the forecast.

What most teams reach for

More inspection and a better tool. Tighten the review cadence, demand more updates, buy a deal-intelligence platform that AI-scores deal health and flags risk.

More inspection on bad data just means more meetings staring at the same mush. AI deal-scoring tools are black boxes sellers learn to distrust or game. The actual signal is already in your CRM: push count. The gap wasn't a missing tool. It was the absence of a forcing function tied to the signal already sitting there.

What this means

The question is not "what tool tells me which deals are bad." It is why the signal already in the CRM didn’t change what happened. That is process and accountability, not something you buy.

How we'd help
  • Run a review that surfaces what the buyer actually said and committed to, instead of restating the forecast.
  • Turn push count into a flare, so deals like the ones holding that $52.5M get worked or killed instead of pushed again.
  • Prioritize what gets captured in the CRM, the real conversation and the next step the buyer owns, so you get true deal visibility rather than mush.
The pain

Sellers do not qualify hard. Junk gets into the pipeline and stays for quarters. You want MEDDPICC to be the discipline that stops it.

What most teams reach for

Install the methodology, build stage gates in Salesforce so a deal cannot advance without the fields filled, train everyone, reinforce with an enablement tool.

It becomes a typing exercise. MEDDPICC as a required field becomes something sellers type to move the deal forward, not judgment they actually apply. A seller enters "Economic Buyer: VP of Ops" because the field is mandatory, not because they have met them. You get clean-looking data you can't trust.

And you will not know if it worked. Methodology rollouts tend to show a first-quarter bump from the attention and the enthusiasm, then slide back to baseline, and you can't tell whether it worked because there was no baseline to measure against. You will have added a process and be hoping.

What this means

A methodology does not fix qualification. What matters is what happens in the conversation, and whether it changes which deals advance and close. The way to know is to measure deals against outcomes before and after the rollout. And since you are rebuilding the stages anyway, that is the one moment to bake the push-count signal into the gates, so the discipline and the warning reinforce each other instead of living in two systems.

How we'd help
  • Define entry and exit qualification for each stage, so a deal cannot sit in a stage it has not earned.
  • Define what a real advance looks like, a thing the buyer did, not a field a seller typed.
  • Bake the warning signals, push count and the missing-conversation tell, into the gates, so the stage structure does the catching instead of relying on inspection.
  • Measure deals against outcomes before and after, so you know whether the new stages changed which deals close rather than hoping they did.
The shape underneath all three

The usual fix for each is to add something. Your data says you already have what you need.

  • The sellers already know which accounts are real.
  • The CRM already knows which deals are dying.
  • The data already knows whether qualification is working.

More leads, more inspection, more tooling, a methodology. Each one adds something instead of reading what is already in front of you. The win or loss already happened, in the conversation, and in data that never made it into the forecast review. That is where the next 90 days are.
